Gender

There are eight genders: masculine, feminine, neuter, unspecified, plural, inanimate, alien, and null.

  • Masculine is used for animate things with a male gender, or specific nouns that are lexically masculine.
  • Feminine is used for animate things with a female gender, or specific nouns that are lexically feminine.
  • Neuter is used for animate things that are without gender (e.g., robots, water bears), or specific nouns that are lexically neuter.
  • Unspecified is used for:
    • Things with an unknown gender,
    • Generic things that could have any gender (e.g., someone),
    • Intentional omition of the gender (often for stylistic reasons),
    • Or where gender is considered irrelevant enough.
    Usage of this is very dependent on dialect (see Dialects).
  • Plural is used for multiple things. It is always used if there is more than one thing, regardless of their genders or lexical genders.
  • Inanimate is always used for things that are not animate, unless the noun specifically uses something else.
  • Alien is used for literal extraterrestrials.
  • Null is used for referring to nothing. In most dialects, using this on a person carries connotations of calling them unimportant.

Vowel Separators

When adding a suffix/prefix to something, it may cause two vowels to be next to each other. This is phonotactically invalid, so a vowel separator is inserted in between the suffix/prefix and what it is being added to. This also applies with syllabic consonants.
What sound this vowel separator is depends on the preceding vowel:

  • If the preceding vowel is /i/, /y/, /e/, /ə/, or a syllabic consonant, it will use the soft vowel separator (SVS), kh [x].
  • If the preceding vowel is /u/, /ɔ/, /a/, /ɔ̃/, or /ã/, it will use the hard vowel separator (HVS), ' [ʔ].

Declension

Nouns are declined by definiteness, case, and gender. Some specific nouns do not get gender marking, and some get gender marking depending on the gender of what it refers to if that is an animate thing (e.g., a cat); the definitions in the Lexicon page will make note of these.

Use

Suffixes/prefixes are stacked onto the noun in this order:
definiteness, noun base, number, case, gender
Below will be explanations on what suffixes to put in these slots.

Definiteness

If the noun is definite, the prefix ħa- is prepended. If it is indefinite, no prefix is added.

Case

Use the case suffixes from above.

Conjugation

Verbs have several qualities, of which they are all conjugated for. These are person, number, gender, tense, mood, polarity, transitivity, and evidentiality. Here is more detail about some of those:

  • Person, number, and gender: Person, number, and gender are polypersonal; they mark both the subject and object. These are both marked in different positions of the suffix group.
    The object is not marked if there is no object; though, due to the fact that every slot has the ability to be empty and communicate meaning that way, the non-transitive suffix is used to clarify the lack of an object.
    Additionally, the person and number are combined into one suffix.
    Unlike gender, the subject and object do not use the same set of suffixes; they each use entirely different sets.
  • Tense: There are 9 tenses: present, immediate past, recent past, remote past, immediate future, remote future, pluperfect, habitual, and future-in-the-past.
  • Mood: There are 8 moods: indicative, infinitive, subjunctive, conditional, imperative, potential, hypothetical, and interrogative. Some moods (subjunctive, imperative, and conditional) conjugate differently based on polarity.
  • Evidentiality: There are 5 types of evidentiality: inferential, nonvisual sensory, visual sensory, assumed, and reportive.

The suffixes/prefixes are in this order:
polarity, base, mood, subject person + number, subject gender, tense, transitivity, object person + number, object gender, optional evidentiality
What to put in each slot will now be listed.

Polarity

If the verb is negative, the prefix ke- is prepended. If it is positive, no prefix is added.
